# How I Work: Strengths, Thinking, and Approach

I've had the chance to reflect on my strengths and how they show up in my work—through structured feedback (CliftonStrengths, development reports) and through years of shipping. This post is a concise picture of how I operate: useful for collaborators, mentees, or anyone who wants to work with me effectively.

## What Drives Me

**Relator.** I do my best work with people I know and trust. I care about deep relationships, not large networks. I'd rather work closely with a small team and deliver than spread thin across many stakeholders. That means I invest in understanding what others need and building trust over time. It also means I'm most engaged when the work is shared—when we're solving something together.

**Input.** I collect information, patterns, and ideas. I build repositories of knowledge—docs, specs, lessons learned—and I use them. I stay current not for its own sake but so I can apply the right idea at the right time. That makes me a good fit for roles where synthesis and "have we seen this before?" matter. The downside: I can over-collect. I have to remind myself to act once I have enough input, not when I have everything.

**Restorative.** I like fixing things. When something is broken—a process, a design, a relationship—I want to figure out what's wrong and fix it. I'm drawn to hard problems and messy situations. That's useful in architecture and in team dynamics: I don't avoid failure; I try to understand it and improve the system. The risk is taking on too much restoration and not delegating or letting go when it's someone else's turn.

**Deliberative.** I'm careful. I think through decisions, anticipate obstacles, and weigh options. I don't jump on the first solution. That reduces risk and improves quality; it can also slow things down if the situation needs speed over perfection. I work best when I have time to think, and I've learned to signal when I need that time versus when "good enough now" is the right call.

**Adaptability.** I can work with the flow. When priorities shift or the plan changes, I adjust. I don't need a fixed roadmap to be productive. That doesn't mean I'm passive—I still push for clarity and direction—but I'm not thrown when the direction changes. Useful in fast-moving environments; I pair it with Deliberative so that "adaptable" doesn't become "no opinion."

## How I Approach Challenges

- **Problems:** I break them down. I look for root cause, not just symptoms. I prefer to fix the system (process, design, contract) so the same failure doesn't repeat.
- **Decisions:** I want enough information to choose, but I don't wait for perfect information. I'm comfortable with "best we can do with what we know" and revisiting if we learn more.
- **Learning:** I learn by doing. I retain little from passive consumption; I need to build, break, and fix to internalize. So I tie learning to projects and real problems.
- **Conflict:** I'd rather resolve than escalate. I look for the underlying interest and see if we can meet it in a way everyone can live with. I don't avoid hard conversations, but I prefer them to be productive.

## What I Need from Others

- **Clarity on intent.** I can work with ambiguity, but I need to know the goal and the constraints. "Figure it out" is fine if the boundaries are clear.
- **Space to think.** When the decision is non-trivial, I need a moment to weigh options. Pushing for an instant answer gets you my first instinct, not my best judgment.
- **Direct feedback.** I'd rather hear "that didn't work because X" than have to guess. I don't take it personally when the feedback is about the work.

## Weaknesses and Mitigations

I can over-collect (Input) and under-act. I mitigate by setting deadlines and by asking "do we have enough to decide?" I can take on too much restoration and burn time on things that aren't mine to fix. I mitigate by checking "is this my problem to solve?" and by delegating. I can be slow when the situation needs speed; I mitigate by naming it—"I need 24 hours to think" or "I can give you a quick answer now and a better one later."
