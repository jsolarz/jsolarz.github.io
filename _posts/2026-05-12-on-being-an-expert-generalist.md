# On Being an Expert Generalist

Martin Fowler and colleagues recently put a name to something I've lived for years: the [Expert Generalist](https://martinfowler.com/articles/expert-generalist.html). Not "broad and shallow," but the ability to learn quickly, spot the fundamentals under the surface of tools and trends, and apply them wherever you land. They argue it's a first-class skill—something to recruit for, develop, and promote. I agree. This post is my take, after the one where I described [how I work](/blog/post.html?slug=how-i-work-strengths-and-approach): why I identify with the label and why it matters more, not less, as the industry pushes for narrower specialization.

## What an Expert Generalist Is (and Isn't)

The caricature of a generalist is the jack-of-all-trades, master of none. The expert generalist is different. You have depth—in fundamentals (patterns, principles, decomposition) and in a few areas you've gone deep in because the work demanded it. You don't know every framework. You know how to learn a new one by recognizing what's familiar: same distributed-systems concerns, different syntax; same volatility-based design problem, different domain. You favor knowledge that ages slowly. Refactoring is refactoring. The core moves of resilience, consistency, and boundaries don't change when the platform does. That's why IDesign and volatility-based decomposition travel with me: they're fundamentals. They work in AWS tooling, in a building management system, in a TUI framework. The surface is different; the structure is the same.

You're also not a lone genius. You collaborate. You know you can't know everything. You ask for help, you work with specialists, and you learn where you can contribute and where you need to step back. That lines up with how I work: Relator (deep collaboration), Input (collect and synthesize), and the humility to assume the odd thing in a new domain is there for a reason before I challenge it.

## Why Customer Focus Matters

Fowler et al. call out customer focus as the lens that keeps curiosity from becoming distraction. If you're curious about everything, you chase every shiny object. If you're curious about what will help the customer (or the user, or the business) excel, you prioritize. I think of it as tying learning to problems. I don't learn a new stack for its own sake; I learn it because we're building something that needs it, or because a certification or a project demands it. Customer focus—or outcome focus—is what makes the generalist effective instead of scattered.

## How It Shows Up for Me

I've worked across Azure and AWS, across solution design and hands-on implementation, across enterprise and side projects. I don't have "five years of Java" or "Kubernetes certified" as my primary identity. I have "I can land in a new codebase or a new domain, map the fundamentals, and contribute—and I know when to bring in a specialist." My deep legs are architecture (volatility, decomposition, contracts), cloud (identity, storage, compute, networking), and a stubborn preference for building to learn. The rest I pick up as needed. That's not a compromise; it's the skill. The industry wants "Azure specialist" or "React specialist." I'd rather be the person who can hold the design together, spot the pattern that applies from somewhere else, and get to a good answer fast—then hand the deep implementation to someone who lives in that stack every day when that's the right move.

## Expert Generalists and AI

Fowler's article notes that expert generalists become more valuable with LLMs in the mix. I see it. AI is great at filling in syntax, boilerplate, and "how do I do X in Y?" The generalist's job is to know what to ask for, how to constrain it (documentation-first, rules that enforce architecture), and when the answer is wrong or off-pattern. The generalist holds the map; the model fills in details. That's the collaboration I want: I own the design and the judgment; the tool accelerates the parts that don't require it.

## Why Name It

If we don't name it, we don't recruit for it, train for it, or promote for it. We default to "we need a specialist in X." Naming "expert generalist" as a skill lets organizations value the person who spans domains, connects the dots, and learns fast—and still expect depth in fundamentals and in a few areas. I'm not saying specialists are obsolete. We need both. I'm saying the generalist is not "failed specialist" or "indecisive." It's a different profile, and it's one I've built my career on. If that's you too, the Fowler article is worth a read—and maybe it's time to stop apologizing for not having a single narrow label.
