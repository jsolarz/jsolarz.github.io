# Solution Architect and Expert Generalist: Why the Role Fits

In [On Being an Expert Generalist](/blog/post.html?slug=on-being-an-expert-generalist) I wrote about the ability to learn quickly, spot fundamentals, and apply them across domains. In [How I Work](/blog/post.html?slug=how-i-work-strengths-and-approach) I described my strengths and how I approach problems. This post connects those to the **solution architect** (or cloud & AI solution engineer) role: why that role is a natural fit for expert generalists and how the competencies line up.

I'm drawing on a strategic blueprint for the Cloud & AI Solution Engineer role—one that spells out core competencies, the blend of technical and relational skills, and the path from strong practitioner to expert. What follows is my take on why the role and the expert-generalist profile align, without tying it to a single employer.

## What the Role Demands

The role typically spans several domains at once:

- **Technical solution design and architecture.** Translate requirements into designs that are scalable, secure, and operable. That means knowing more than one slice of the stack: identity, compute, storage, networking, data, and increasingly AI/ML. You don't have to be the deepest specialist in each, but you need to know how they fit together and when to bring in a specialist.
- **Customer engagement and advisory.** You're a trusted technical advisor. That means listening, understanding unstated needs, and communicating clearly. You articulate value and trade-offs in terms the customer cares about—outcomes, risk, cost—not just features.
- **Problem-solving and innovation.** You run into ill-defined or novel problems. You build and evaluate proofs of concept. You help decide what's viable and what's not. That requires curiosity and a willingness to go deep enough to have an opinion, then step back when someone else should own the detail.
- **Project leadership and collaboration.** You coordinate across internal and customer teams. You don't do everything yourself; you orchestrate. That requires knowing where you add value and where you enable others.
- **Continuous learning.** The stack changes. New services, new patterns, new constraints. The role assumes you stay current and apply what you learn to real solutions.
- **DevOps and operational thinking.** Solutions need to be deployable, observable, and maintainable. CI/CD, automation, and operational readiness are part of the design, not an afterthought.

No one person is the best in the world at all of these. The role rewards people who can **span** them: hold the end-to-end picture, go deep where it matters, and collaborate with specialists when that's the right move. That's the expert generalist profile.

## How Expert Generalist Traits Map to the Role

From [Fowler's article](https://martinfowler.com/articles/expert-generalist.html): curiosity, collaborativeness, customer focus, favoring fundamental knowledge, and a blend of generalist and specialist skills.

- **Curiosity** drives the continuous learning the role requires. New tech, new domains, new customer contexts—you're expected to get up to speed and contribute. Curiosity also means not stopping at "it works"; you want to understand why and when it breaks.
- **Collaborativeness** is how you avoid the trap of needing to know everything. You work with specialists, you ask for help, you integrate their input into the design. The role is inherently collaborative; the generalist thrives by knowing when to lead and when to follow.
- **Customer focus** is the lens that keeps curiosity from scattering. You learn what helps the customer excel. You prioritize design and advice that delivers outcome and value, not what's merely interesting.
- **Favoring fundamentals** (patterns, principles, decomposition) is what lets you move across services and domains. The solution architect who only knows one platform's quirks is stuck. The one who knows resilience, identity, data consistency, and boundaries can translate across Azure, AWS, GCP, and on-prem.
- **Blend of generalist and specialist** matches the role's expectation: broad across the solution, deep in a few areas. You're not "only" a generalist; you have depth where the job or your past work demanded it. That depth is what makes your broad judgment credible.

So the role doesn't just tolerate expert generalists; it **selects for** them. Narrow specialists are essential, but the person who holds the design together, connects the dots, and advises the customer is usually the one with the generalist profile.

## What I Take From the Blueprint

A strategic blueprint for this role (the one I used as reference) emphasizes: technical depth in cloud, data, and AI; behavioral competencies like credibility, self-awareness, collaboration, and openness to different perspectives; and a path that combines targeted upskilling with intentional development of how you work with others. The details are employer-specific, but the shape is universal.

For me, the useful part is naming the combination: **technical breadth and depth + customer and stakeholder impact + continuous learning + collaboration.** That's the solution architect's job. It's also the expert generalist's job. If you see yourself in the "I span domains, I hold the design, I learn fast and I work with specialists" description, the solution architect role is one of the places you belong. You're not a failed specialist; you're in the role that rewards the profile you have.
